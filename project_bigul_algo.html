1. Project Overview

Background & Business Context

Algorithmic trading had always existed at Bigul, but exclusively through a raw API for developers who could write their own Python strategies — a segment representing under 5% of the user base. Meanwhile, competitor platforms were launching no-code strategy builders and capturing exactly the retail segment Bigul wanted to retain and monetize further.

The business opportunity was clear: retail users who adopt automated strategies trade more consistently and generate more transaction revenue. The risk was equally clear — handing real capital control to a "set and forget" system, used by people without a trading background, was a serious responsibility that could damage trust if it went wrong even once.

Goals & Objectives:

  • Make strategy creation accessible without requiring any coding knowledge.
  • Build trust through transparency — no strategy should feel like a black box.
  • Prevent catastrophic losses through hard, user-visible risk guardrails.

Target Audience

Two segments needed very different levels of hand-holding:

Busy Professionals (58%)

Working professionals who can't watch the market all day but want rule-based exposure to it — Bigul's primary growth target for this feature.

Existing API Traders (42%)

Power users already running scripted strategies via API Connect, who wanted a faster way to prototype ideas before writing code for them.

2. My Role & Collaboration

I owned the strategy-builder UX and the visual language for representing trading logic to non-technical users — arguably the hardest translation problem I've worked on at Bigul.

  • Risk Analyst: Defining the hard guardrails (max capital per strategy, daily loss circuit-breaker) that would exist regardless of what a user configured.
  • Quant Engineering: Understanding which trading conditions could realistically be expressed as visual rules versus which needed to stay code-only for the advanced tier.
  • Product Manager: Sequencing the release so a template library shipped first, with fully custom rule-building unlocked only after a user had run one guided strategy.
  • Legal & Compliance: Reviewing all "backtest" language to make sure past-performance disclaimers were prominent and unavoidable, not buried in fine print.

User Persona & Key Pain Points

Meet Karan, a 32-year-old IT professional who wants rule-based exposure to the market but has never written a line of trading code:

01
Fear of Losing Control

Karan worried an automated strategy could keep trading and losing money while he was in a meeting, with no way to intervene quickly.

02
Black-Box Distrust

Without seeing how a strategy had performed historically, Karan had no basis to trust it with real capital, so he simply never activated one.

03
Overwhelming Configuration

Early concepts that exposed every possible trading condition upfront left Karan unsure which options actually mattered for his goal.

Product Thinking Insight:

Adoption wasn't blocked by complexity alone — it was blocked by a lack of a safe way to build confidence first. Users needed to see a strategy "work" on historical data and know exactly how to stop it before they'd ever risk real money on it.

4. Problem Definition

The Challenges & Constraints

Every visual rule needed to map to a real, executable trading condition without oversimplifying it into something misleading. And no matter how a strategy was configured, platform-level guardrails had to remain in force — a design choice, not a user setting.

70%+ Target backtest-to-live conversion
< 5 min First strategy creation goal
0 Guardrail-bypass incidents allowed

5. Design Process

I started from the trading logic itself — mapping the most common retail strategy patterns (moving average crossovers, price-threshold triggers, stop-loss ladders) into plain-English condition templates before designing any visual chrome.

Fig 1. Strategy flow from template selection through backtest review to live activation.

From Low-Fidelity Wireframes to Iterations

The first version presented a blank canvas where users assembled conditions freely — testers stared at it for minutes with no idea where to start. We pivoted to a template-first model: choose a starting strategy pattern, then customize its specific thresholds.

Fig 2. Wireframe iteration moving from a blank canvas to a guided, template-first builder.
Design Rationale:

Templates before blank canvases: Every tester who started from a template completed a working strategy; almost none who started from a blank canvas did. Flexibility was still available — just one step later, after a first success built confidence.

6. Visual Design System

Algo extended Apex with a new "condition chip" component language — each trading rule renders as a plain-language, color-coded chip rather than a code block or raw form field.

Fig 3. Condition-chip components, backtest chart overlays, and guardrail indicator patterns.

Design Tokens Overview

Condition Chips

Each rule reads like a sentence — "If price drops 3% below 20-day average" — rendered as an editable, color-coded chip rather than raw parameters.

Backtest Overlay

A shaded chart region showing simulated entries and exits directly on real historical price data, not an abstract summary table.

Guardrail Indicators

A persistent, non-dismissible badge showing the active capital cap and daily loss limit on every strategy screen.

WCAG 2.1 Accessibility Considerations:

  • Chart Alternatives: Every backtest chart ships with a plain-text summary (win rate, max drawdown, total trades) for screen-reader users.
  • Focus Order: The kill-switch control is reachable within two keyboard tabs from anywhere in the strategy dashboard.

7. High-Fidelity Walkthrough

Visual Strategy Composer

Users assemble a strategy from plain-language condition chips ("if," "and," "then") instead of writing code, with each chip validated in real time against the selected instrument's data.

The Solution: A guided, template-first builder where the first strategy is pre-populated and only requires adjusting two or three thresholds, with an "Advanced" toggle for fully custom rules.

Backtest-to-Live Confirmation Gate

Before any strategy can trade real money, users must review its historical performance — including its worst historical drawdown, not just its best-case return.

The Solution: A mandatory summary screen showing win rate, max drawdown, and total simulated trades, with the "Go Live" button disabled until the user has scrolled through and explicitly acknowledged the risk summary.

8. Product Strategy & Trade-offs

Every automation feature in fintech is a negotiation between flexibility, safety, and how much a first-time user can absorb at once.

Flexibility vs. Guided Simplicity

The Conflict: Power users wanted full custom rule-building from day one; first-time automation users needed heavy guidance or they'd abandon the feature entirely.

The Strategy: Template-first for everyone, with an "Advanced" mode that unlocks after completing one guided strategy. Power users reach full flexibility in under a minute; newcomers never see it until they're ready.

MVP Phase vs Future Roadmap

The MVP shipped five strategy templates covering the most common retail patterns. The roadmap includes community-shared strategy templates (with performance transparency) and multi-condition strategies that combine signals across instruments.

9. Challenges & Solutions

Challenge: Beta users activated strategies without reviewing backtests

The Problem: In the first beta cohort, several users tapped straight through the backtest screen without reading it, then were surprised by losses within a strategy's normal, historically-expected drawdown range.

Design Iteration: We changed the backtest summary from a passive screen into an active confirmation gate — the "Go Live" button stayed disabled until the user scrolled through the full drawdown chart and tapped an explicit "I understand the risk" acknowledgment.

The Result: Post-launch support queries about "unexpected" losses dropped substantially, and exit surveys showed users felt more informed going in.

13. Key Learnings

Automating a decision that involves someone's money surfaced lessons well beyond visual design.

01
Guardrails increase adoption, not just safety

Making the capital cap visible and non-dismissible didn't scare users away — it was the reason many first-time automation users felt safe enough to try the feature at all.

02
Templates are a trust-building device

A working first strategy in under five minutes did more to build confidence than any amount of explanatory copy about how the builder worked.

03
Confirmation gates beat passive disclaimers

An "I understand the risk" screen users must actively engage with changes behavior in a way a footnote disclaimer never will.
