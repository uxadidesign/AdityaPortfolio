1. Project Overview

Background & Business Context

Every Indian brokerage is bound by SEBI's KYC regulations — PAN verification, address proof, bank account linking, a signature, and increasingly, video-based in-person verification (V-CIP). None of these steps are negotiable. What's negotiable is how much friction, confusion, and anxiety a user experiences while completing them.

Within the broader Bigul Trading App redesign, KYC consistently surfaced as the single biggest drop-off point — worth pulling out as its own dedicated initiative rather than a line item inside a larger release, precisely because compliance requirements meant we couldn't simply remove steps to fix it.

Goals & Objectives:

  • Reduce document-related abandonment by making capture foolproof on the first attempt.
  • Cut manual rejection rates caused by illegible signatures and blurry uploads.
  • Replace compliance jargon with plain-language explanations at every step.

Target Audience

KYC drop-off wasn't evenly distributed — it concentrated in one segment:

First-Time Investors (68%)

Users opening their first-ever demat account, unfamiliar with terms like "DIS," "In-Person Verification," or why a bank statement was even required.

Re-attempting Users (32%)

Users who had failed KYC once already and were re-entering the flow frustrated, often unsure exactly what had gone wrong the first time.

2. My Role & Collaboration

I led UX for the compliance flow end-to-end, working unusually closely with our in-house compliance officer since every screen change had regulatory implications.

  • Compliance Officer: Reviewing every microcopy change against SEBI guidelines to make sure "plain language" never drifted into legally inaccurate territory.
  • Backend & OCR Vendor: Defining the confidence thresholds at which auto-extracted PAN and address data would be trusted versus flagged for manual review.
  • Customer Support Lead: Mining six months of rejection-related support transcripts to identify the exact phrases causing the most confusion.
  • Product Manager: Balancing the desire to add steps for clarity against a hard internal ceiling on total onboarding time.

3. Research & Discovery

I combined support-ticket analysis with moderated usability sessions where participants completed KYC on their own phones while thinking aloud — this surfaced friction that clickstream data alone couldn't explain.

User Persona & Key Pain Points

Meet Ananya, a 24-year-old first-jobber opening her first demat account with her first salary bonus. She represents our largest KYC segment:

01
No Capture Guidance

Ananya photographed her PAN card at an angle with glare on it. The app accepted the blurry image without warning, only rejecting it two days later.

02
Opaque Rejection Reasons

Her rejection notice read "Document verification failed" with no indication of what specifically was wrong or how to fix it.

03
Fear of Repeating the Mistake

Without knowing what failed, Ananya hesitated to re-upload anything, worried she'd waste another multi-day review cycle.

Product Thinking Insight:

The real fix wasn't a better rejection message after the fact — it was preventing the bad capture from ever entering the pipeline. Real-time validation at the point of capture would eliminate most rejections before they happened.

4. Problem Definition

The Challenges & Constraints

We could not skip or shorten any regulatory step, and OCR confidence thresholds had to stay conservative enough to satisfy the compliance team, who were understandably wary of automating away a manual review step entirely.

+22% Target KYC completion
< 3% Rejection rate goal
< 6 mins Average completion time

5. Design Process

I mapped the entire regulatory journey and identified exactly where real-time validation could intercept an error before submission, rather than after.

Fig 1. Capture-to-verification flow showing where real-time validation was inserted ahead of submission.

From Low-Fidelity Wireframes to Iterations

Early wireframes combined document capture and digital signature on one scrollable screen. Usability sessions showed users skimming past the signature box entirely, assuming it was decorative next to the document preview.

Fig 2. Wireframe iteration separating capture and signature into distinct, sequential steps.
Design Rationale:

One task per screen: Splitting capture and signature into two dedicated screens, each with its own real-time validation, meant a slightly longer flow but a much clearer mental model of "one thing to get right at a time."

6. Visual Design System

This flow reused Bigul's Apex design system, extended with capture-specific components: a live framing guide overlay, inline validation states, and a plain-language error library reviewed by compliance.

Fig 3. Capture guide overlay states and the plain-language error message library.

Design Tokens Overview

Capture Guide Overlay

A live corner-bracket frame that turns green only when glare, blur, and cropping checks all pass — before the shutter is even pressed.

Plain-Language Errors

Every rejection reason was rewritten in one sentence a non-finance user could act on immediately, reviewed line-by-line with compliance.

Progress Transparency

A persistent step tracker so users always know exactly how many regulatory steps remain and roughly how long each takes.

WCAG 2.1 Accessibility Considerations:

  • Alternative Input: A type-to-sign fallback exists for users who cannot complete a smooth finger-drawn signature.
  • Screen Reader Support: Capture guide states announce pass/fail conditions audibly, not just through the visual green/red frame.

7. High-Fidelity Walkthrough

Camera-Guided Document Capture

Instead of accepting any photo a user submitted, the camera view now overlays a framing guide that checks focus, glare, and cropping in real time before allowing capture.

The Solution: A traffic-light border around the live camera preview — red until all quality checks pass, green with a haptic tap the instant the frame is submission-ready.

Type-to-Sign Fallback

Finger-drawn signatures on a small screen were the single largest cause of manual rejection. Forcing everyone through a better drawing tool wouldn't fully solve it.

The Solution: A stylized type-to-sign component that renders an official-looking script signature, offered as an equally valid, clearly explained alternative alongside the draw option.

8. Product Strategy & Trade-offs

Regulatory UX means constantly negotiating between what compliance requires and what users can realistically tolerate.

Automation Trust vs. Manual Review

The Conflict: Fully automating document approval would speed things up, but compliance wasn't ready to remove human review entirely for edge cases.

The Strategy: High-confidence OCR matches auto-approve instantly; anything below the threshold routes to manual review with the specific mismatch highlighted for the reviewer, rather than a blanket "needs review" flag. Speed improved without asking compliance to give up their safety net.

MVP Phase vs Future Roadmap

The MVP focused on capture validation and plain-language rejections. The roadmap includes extending real-time validation to video-KYC (V-CIP) sessions, where lighting and framing issues currently cause a similar pattern of avoidable failures.

9. Challenges & Solutions

Challenge: High digital signature rejection rate

The Problem: Over 15% of KYC applications were rejected because users signed with clumsy finger swipes on mobile, producing illegible squiggles that compliance couldn't accept.

Design Iteration: We introduced the type-to-sign fallback alongside an improved draw canvas with pressure-smoothing, and explained both options with a one-line comparison so users could choose confidently.

The Result: Signature-related rejections fell from 15% to 2.4% within two weeks of launch.

13. Key Learnings

Compliance UX has its own rules, and this project taught me to respect them without treating them as an excuse for a bad experience.

01
Prevent errors, don't just explain them

Every hour spent on rejection-message copywriting was worth less than the real-time capture guide that stopped the bad photo from being submitted in the first place.

02
Offer alternatives instead of forcing one interaction

Type-to-sign didn't replace the draw canvas — it sat beside it. Giving users a second valid path removed friction without removing choice.

03
Compliance officers are a design resource, not a blocker

Involving compliance in copy review from day one meant we never had to walk back a "friendly" rewrite that turned out to be legally inaccurate.
