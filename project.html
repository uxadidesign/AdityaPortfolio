1. Project Overview

Background & Business Context

Bigul.co is a retail trading platform aimed at making capital market investments accessible to regular retail users. In a highly saturated fintech space, user onboarding and speed-to-trade are the ultimate competitive advantages.

Before the redesign, Bigul.co was facing severe drop-offs during its mandatory regulatory onboarding (KYC) process, and novice traders were struggling to execute basic orders due to a cluttered web/mobile interface designed around professional trader specifications.

Goals & Objectives:

  • Increase KYC funnel conversion by streamlining registration steps and decreasing form fatigue.
  • Improve order placement speed for first-time traders.
  • Decrease customer support load linked to confusing onboarding requirements.

Target Audience

Our primary users fall into two categories:

Novice Investors (65%)

Users aged 21-35 trying to grow their savings. They feel easily intimidated by complex charts, stock terminal jargon, and heavy tax documents.

Active Retail Traders (35%)

Experienced users demanding speed, reliable order routing, extensive indicators, and advanced chart tools without UI lag.

2. My Role & Collaboration

As the Lead UI/UX Designer on the project, I held complete ownership of the UX strategy, customer research, visual design, and developer handoff.

Fintech products cannot be built in design isolation. I worked closely with:

  • Product Managers: Aligning regulatory compliance goals with interactive screen milestones to ensure zero compliance gaps while maintaining usability.
  • Engineering Team: Standardizing visual components into a scalable library. Working with front-end engineers to implement CSS layout systems, resulting in pixel-perfect rendering.
  • QA Engineers: Creating UX validation checklist states (error cases, network lag alerts, slow response screens) during design QA.
  • Stakeholders: Reviewing early mockups to balance trading risk mitigation with rapid trade conversions.

3. Research & Discovery

To understand where users were dropping off, we initiated a dual research pathway: quantitative analysis of clickstream data combined with qualitative remote usability sessions.

User Persona & Key Pain Points

Meet Rohan, a 26-year-old software engineer representing our novice segment. Rohan wants to invest in tech stocks but is blocked by three main friction points:

01
Documentation Overhead

KYC required uploading multiple physical documents, leading to instant abandonment when Rohan didn't have papers handy.

02
Jargon Overload

Terms like "DIS", "Power of Attorney", and "Margin limits" were dropped into step flows without contextual explanation.

03
Trading Terminal Anxiety

Novice users feared making mistakes and losing money because buttons like "Buy" vs "Short Sell" were visually identical.

Product Thinking Insight:

We realized users were not dropping off because of lazy UI styling; they dropped off due to **cognitive load** and **anxiety**. Our redesign had to focus on progressive disclosure—only showing complex settings when users requested them.

The Challenges & Constraints

We had to comply with strict regulatory guidelines (KYC verification required validating physical signatures, bank verifications, and live photo check). We couldn't eliminate these steps, but we could change *how* users experienced them.

5. Design Process

We mapped out a revamped user flow that broken down the 18 steps into 5 distinct visual categories. Below is the simplified Information Architecture and User Flow designed to eliminate loopbacks.

Fig 1. User flow mapping showing progressive disclosure paths for trading configuration.

From Low-Fidelity Wireframes to Iterations

We tested early sketches with a cohort of 15 users. Initially, we put bank account linking and digital signature in one scrollable view. However, users were overwhelmed by seeing bank details and signature boxes together.

Fig 2. Low-Fidelity wireframes iterating on step-by-step onboarding wizard.
Design Rationale:

Decoupling Steps: We split bank linking and signature collection into two separate screens with auto-validation. Although this added 1 extra click, it eliminated the form-abandonment rate by 34% in user tests because each step felt quick and clear.

6. Visual Design System

To ensure design consistency and speed up development, we established **Apex**, a centralized Design System tailored to high-density financial layouts.

Fig 3. Apex Design System guide showing components, typography rules, and theme variables.

Design Tokens Overview

Typography

Outfit (Headings) for modern look & Inter (Body / Numbers) for geometric spacing readability.

Color Palette

Deep Navy backgrounds (`#0a0e17`) paired with electric blue (`#3b82f6`) and rich emerald green (`#10b981`) for buy actions.

Grid & Spacing

8px baseline grid to ensure alignment across complex data tables and interactive charts.

WCAG 2.1 Accessibility Considerations:

  • Contrast Ratios: All financial textual data (price quotes, yields) meets a minimum 4.5:1 ratio against background.
  • Color Independence: Price movements are never shown solely through green/red color shifts; secondary icon identifiers (up/down chevron indicators) accompany value adjustments.

7. High-Fidelity Walkthrough

Below are the final designs addressing core usability drop-offs in the terminal and onboarding flows:

The Simplified Quick-Trade Pad

Novice users frequently complained about making trade errors in the original layout because the trade panel looked identical to advanced analytics panels.

The Solution: A isolated, slide-out drawer featuring a clear card layout separating Buy and Sell options with distinctive, contrasting header highlights.

Auto-extract KYC Uploads

Instead of forcing manual entry of 16 bank account digits, we integrated instant UPI verification and camera OCR to scan tax cards automatically.

The Solution: Users simply take a photo, and our app instantly fills in the required field lines, reducing typing friction by 80%.

8. Product Strategy & Trade-offs

Designing digital products requires knowing what to leave out. We had to negotiate crucial trade-offs between business requirements and user preferences.

User Desire vs. Regulatory Necessity

The Conflict: Users wanted to start trading immediately without submitting KYC. Regulatory compliance demanded verification before any capital trade.

The Strategy: We introduced "Simulated Paper Trading" as the default state for unverified accounts. Users could download the app and trade fake credits immediately, experiencing the product value. Once they attempted to place a real order, we dynamically routed them to the KYC sequence.

MVP Phase vs Future Roadmap

For the MVP, we focused entirely on simplifying the onboarding funnel and quick trade placement. The future roadmap includes introducing AI-guided portfolio balancing tools and advanced charting features for power users.

9. Challenges & Solutions

Challenge: High digital signature rejection rate

The Problem: Over 15% of KYC applications were rejected manually by backend compliance officers because users signed with clumsy finger swipes on mobile, resulting in illegible squiggles.

Design Iteration: We built an alternative fallback tool. Users could either use a simplified type-to-sign component (generating an official digital script) or upload a photo of a physical signature.

The Result: Rejections fell from 15% to 2.4% within two weeks of launch.

Key Lessons Learned:

Sometimes, adding step count leads to higher confidence. Breaking a giant regulatory form into quick, bite-sized tasks is significantly better than cramming it all into one page, even if the user has to click more times. Focus on *perceived* friction, not just actual clicks.
